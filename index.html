A Proclamation of Blood and Memory

Let us speak plainly, without the whispering. Let us shout what the river, the soil, and the bones of our ancestors already know: The Anioma are Igbo.

Not by political concession. Not by cultural accident. But by the immutable laws of origin—by blood, by tongue, by spirit, and by the undeniable truth that echoes in every name, every prayer, every proverb spoken west of the Niger.

Who hears the name Ngozichukwuka—"God's blessing is greater"—and dares to say it is not Igbo? What spirit animates that name if not the Igbo spirit? Who stands in the market in Asaba, haggles with a woman from Onitsha in seamless understanding, and claims they are not speaking the same language of commerce, kinship, and life? This is not coincidence. This is inheritance.

IGBO BU OFU
(Igbo is One.)

Igbo bu ofu. Igbo is one. This is not a slogan for banners; it is the foundational truth upon which our entire reality is built. A tree may have branches that stretch toward different skies, but they draw life from the same root. To deny the root is to condemn the branch to a slow, bewildering death.

The Evidence That Does Not Lie

1. The Onomastic Test: Our Names Betray Us

Our names are not just identifiers; they are testaments of origin. They are philosophical statements, spiritual contracts, and historical records woven into syllables.

  • Chukwuemeka – "God has done great"
  • Nwabugwu – "Child is precious"
  • Akachukwu – "Hand of God"
  • Chinedu – "God leads"
  • Ifeanyichukwu – "Nothing is impossible with God"
  • Obiajulu – "The heart is at peace"

These are not Edo names. They are not Urhobo names. They are profoundly, inarguably Igbo names. They are built on Igbo theophoric elements (Chukwu, Chi, Chukwuka) and Igbo worldview. You cannot answer to such a name, bear its meaning and destiny, and claim alienation from the people who birthed its meaning. A name is a flag planted in the soil of an identity. Ours fly the Igbo colors.

2. The Linguistic Unity: One Language, Many Melodies

Language is the soul of a people made audible. When an Ndokwa elder tells a folktale about the tortoise, and a child from Nsukka understands every twist, every moral, every rhythmic pause—that is not "cultural similarity." That is shared soul.

"Enuani is to Igbo what American English is to British English. Different accent, same language. Anyone claiming otherwise is pushing politics, not linguistics."
– Professor Emeka Nwadiorah, Linguist

The Enuani dialect is not a separate language. It is a dialect of Igbo, as Yoruba spoken in Ibadan is to that spoken in Akure. The core vocabulary—the words for father (nna), mother (nne), life (ndu), and death (ọnwụ)—are identical. The grammatical structure, the proverbs, the tonal system that gives the language its music, are the same.

"How can you understand a people completely, and they you, yet claim to be foreign to them?"

How can you understand a people completely, and they you, yet claim to be foreign to them? Such a thing does not exist in anthropology or in human feeling. Understanding is the offspring of kinship.

3. The Ritual Concord: One Spirit, One Practice

Go to the heart of any Anioma community during the Iwa Ji festival. Watch the breaking of the kola nut—the presentation, the invocation, the intricate prayer that calls upon the ancestors and Ala, the Earth Goddess. Then go to Anambra, to Imo, to Ebonyi. You will witness the same ritual.

That we practice them, with the same reverence and the same forms, is the most potent proof that we are not neighbors looking in. We are family within the house.

The Historical Timeline: Our Journey

Pre-1900

Ancestral Migration

Anioma people trace ancestry to Igbo heartland migrations across the Niger. Cultural and linguistic ties remain unbroken.

1946

The 1946 Petition

Anioma leaders formally petition for reunification with Eastern Region, declaring: "We are culturally, linguistically and historically Igbo."

1967-1970

The Biafran War & Trauma

The Asaba Massacre and war trauma create survival mechanisms of identity distancing as protection.

1991

Delta State Creation

Anioma becomes part of Delta State, further administratively separated from Igbo kin in the Southeast.

Today

The Reawakening

A new generation seeks to reclaim identity, heal historical wounds, and complete the reunification dream of 1946.

"We are culturally, linguistically and historically Igbo. We belong with our kin in the East."
– 1946 Anioma Petition to British Colonial Government

They felt the alienation of being administratively severed from their life-source. They recognized the political marginalization that came from being a cultural minority in a foreign regional structure. They begged for reunification. They knew who they were.

We dishonor their memory, their clarity, and their courage when we today stumble over the identity they proclaimed without fear. Their plea from 1946 is not a dusty relic; it is an unanswered letter, a door left swinging open, waiting for us to finally walk through.

The Wound of 1967 and the Survival Lie

Yes, the war changed everything. The Asaba Massacre taught a horrific lesson: that to be openly Igbo on the wrong side of an artificial line could be a death sentence. In the aftermath of such trauma, the psyche does what it must to survive. It learns to hide. It manufactures distance. It whispers, "Perhaps we are not exactly them, for our own safety."

OVER 1 MILLION LIVES LOST.

This survival mechanism is understandable, even heartbreaking. But we must recognize it for what it is: a protective fiction born of profound pain. It is a scar, not a feature.

"To mistake the scar for the original skin is to allow the perpetrators of that trauma a final, lasting victory—the victory of making us deny ourselves."

The war sought to destroy the Igbo spirit. Let us not complete that work with our own hands and tongues.

A Call to Wholeness: How We Come Home

Coming home is not a physical migration. It is a psychological and cultural reintegration. It is a return to truth.

Speak It Unashamed: Let us end the code-switching of identity. In Lagos, in Abuja, abroad, the answer to "Where are you from?" is "I am Igbo, from Asaba in Delta State." No hesitation. No explanatory footnote.

Teach the Truth: Let our children learn in schools that Enuani is a dialect of Igbo. Let their history books tell them of the 1946 petition, of Nnebisi's journey from Nteje, of our shared cosmology with our eastern kin.

Re-knit the Bonds: Let us actively rebuild the social, economic, and marital bridges across the Niger. Let Anioma businesses integrate with the Igbo entrepreneurial network. Let festivals be jointly celebrated.

Demand Political Recognition: Let our representatives begin the sober, constitutional work of advocating for our cultural reality to be reflected in administrative reality. Let the ghost of 1946 finally be laid to rest through action.

A Story of the Writer

My journey to understanding began with my name. Uzor Blessing Ngozi—four names, each carrying the weight of Igbo history and spirituality. Growing up in Asaba, I spoke Enuani at home, learned English in school, and heard whispers about "our differences" from the East.

But the whispers never matched the reality. When I visited my cousins in Onitsha, we understood each other perfectly. When we celebrated festivals, our rituals mirrored theirs. When we prayed, we called upon the same Chi.

The contradiction haunted me. How could we be so alike yet constantly told we were different? My search led me to the 1946 archives, to linguistics research, to elders' stories. I discovered our grandfathers had already answered the question we still struggle with today.

This article is not just research—it's personal. It's the reconciliation of my Delta State certificate with my Igbo soul. It's the healing of a fracture that should never have existed. I write so my children won't have to explain why their names don't match their story. I write so we can all come home.

This is not an argument. It is a remembrance.
