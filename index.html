Chapter I: The Proclamation

Let us speak plainly, without the whispering. Let us state what the river, the soil, and the bones of our ancestors already know: The Anioma are Igbo.

Not by political concession. Not by cultural accident. But by the immutable laws of origin‚Äîby blood, by tongue, by spirit, and by the undeniable truth that echoes in every name, every prayer, every proverb spoken west of the Niger.

Who hears the name Ngozichukwuka‚Äî"God's blessing is greater"‚Äîand dares to say it is not Igbo? What spirit animates that name if not the Igbo spirit? Who stands in the market in Asaba, haggles with a woman from Onitsha in seamless understanding, and claims they are not speaking the same language of commerce, kinship, and life? This is not coincidence. This is inheritance.

Igbo bu ofu. Igbo is one. This is not a slogan for banners; it is the foundational truth upon which our entire reality is built. A tree may have branches that stretch toward different skies, but they draw life from the same root. To deny the root is to condemn the branch to a slow, bewildering death.

Ours fly the Igbo colors.

The 1946 Petition

History holds a mirror to our present confusion. In 1946, our grandfathers‚Äîunburdened by the trauma of a war yet to come‚Äîlifted a collective voice so clear it shattered ambiguity.

They did not write a polite request. They issued a declaration of being.

"We are culturally, linguistically and historically Igbo. We belong with our kin in the East."
‚Äì 1946 Anioma Petition to British Colonial Government

They felt the alienation of being administratively severed from their life-source. They recognized the political marginalization that came from being a cultural minority in a foreign regional structure. They begged for reunification. They knew who they were.

We dishonor their memory, their clarity, and their courage when we today stumble over the identity they proclaimed without fear. Their plea from 1946 is not a dusty relic; it is an unanswered letter, a door left swinging open, waiting for us to finally walk through.

The Trauma of 1967

Yes, the war changed everything. The Asaba Massacre taught a horrific lesson: that to be openly Igbo on the wrong side of an artificial line could be a death sentence. In the aftermath of such trauma, the psyche does what it must to survive. It learns to hide. It manufactures distance. It whispers, "Perhaps we are not exactly them, for our own safety."

To mistake the scar for the original skin is to allow the perpetrators of that trauma a final, lasting victory‚Äîthe victory of making us deny ourselves.

This survival mechanism is understandable, even heartbreaking. But we must recognize it for what it is: a protective fiction born of profound pain. It is a scar, not a feature.

Chapter IV: The Call to Remembrance

The living continuity of Anioma culture within the broader Igbo world.

Coming home is not a physical migration. It is a psychological and cultural reintegration. It is a return to truth.

Pathways to Wholeness

1. Speak It Unashamed: Let us end the code-switching of identity. In Lagos, in Abuja, abroad, the answer to "Where are you from?" is "I am Igbo, from Asaba in Delta State." No hesitation. No explanatory footnote.

2. Teach the Truth: Let our children learn that Enuani is a dialect of Igbo. Let their history books tell them of the 1946 petition, of our shared cosmology with our eastern kin.

3. Re-knit the Bonds: Let us actively rebuild the social, economic, and marital bridges across the Niger. Let Anioma businesses integrate with the Igbo entrepreneurial network.

4. Demand Recognition: Let our representatives advocate for our cultural reality to be reflected in administrative reality.

"The River Joins, It Does Not Divide. The Niger River is not our border. It is our shared landmark. It does not separate Igbo land; it flows through it."

Igbo bu ofu. We are one people. From Abakaliki to Aboh. From Owerri to Onicha-Olona. The language is one. The blood is one. The destiny is one.

Our fathers wanted unity. Our grandfathers cried for it in 1946. The very names we answer to demand it. The spirits of our ancestors, who speak to us in the Igbo tongue through divination and dream, expect it.

Let us no longer be the branch that denies the root. Let us be the branch that strengthens the tree. Let the song we sing‚Äîacross the waters, from the Good Land to the heartland‚Äîbe the same song, in the same language, with one unstoppable, united voice.

Uzor Blessing Ngozi
Writer & Cultural Historian
